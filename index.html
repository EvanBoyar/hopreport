Notes on methodtap to open

Each band's score is the product of four gates, so a single bad factor is enough to close a band: score = 100 × MUF × absorption × geomagnetic × season. The MUF gate uses the nearest fresh ionosonde's measured MUF(3000) when one is reachable through prop.kc2g.com, and otherwise falls back to an estimate built from SFI. A band scores full marks below roughly 0.85 of the MUF and falls off sharply above it. The absorption gate models D-layer loss from the sun's elevation at your grid, scaled by 1/f² and multiplied by the current GOES X-ray flare class, which is what closes 160 through 40 m in daylight and opens them at night. The geomagnetic gate lowers the MUF by 5% for each Kp point above 3 and applies a direct penalty scaled to your latitude, weighted toward the high bands. The season gate trims the low bands for summer static. Treat the 6 m figure as a floor rather than a forecast, since nothing in the indices predicts sporadic E. The LUF readout is derived from the same absorption gate rather than modeled separately: it is the frequency where daytime D-layer loss alone would pull a band below fair. It is shown for orientation and never feeds the scores a second time, since each band's absorption factor already contains it. A real LUF also depends on your power, antenna, and mode. Weak-signal modes such as FT8 dig well below the figure shown, which is why live spots can appear under it. When the LUF climbs past the MUF during a strong flare, the page flags a shortwave fadeout.

The live layer subscribes to PSK Reporter's public MQTT feed over WebSockets, filtered to spots transmitted from or received in your grid square and its eight neighbors, kept for a rolling hour, aged by when each spot was heard rather than when it arrived. The feed carries no history, so the window fills gradually after the page opens. Once a band collects three or more spots, its displayed score becomes 0.6 live + 0.4 model, where the live score combines spot count with maximum distance normalized for each band. The spot count is first weighed against expected operator activity for the local solar time and day of week, since the networks go quiet in the small hours and run hot on contest weekends: a handful of spots at 4 am counts for more than the same handful at 8 pm. The activity curve is an estimate (trough near 4 am at about 15% of the evening peak, weekends about 35% busier); the reporting networks chart these cycles but publish no hard ratios. Major contest weekends raise that bar much further, per mode: a flood of CW spots during CQ WW CW is business as usual, not an opening, and Field Day is the classic everything-looks-open trap. The contest dates are computed from their own rules (fourth full weekend of June and the like), which have been stable for decades and outlive any calendar feed this page could fetch. SSB-only majors are ignored since phone barely reaches this feed, and regional events like Field Day apply only when your grid is in the Americas. An active contest is shown under the title. Measured reality outvotes the prediction in both directions. CW spots arrive through the same feed because PSK Reporter ingests the Reverse Beacon Network's skimmer spots. The count switches above the log choose what feeds each score: digital spots, CW spots, and the computed model can be mixed freely, and with the model excluded a band shows no verdict until it collects three live spots. The feed skews toward FT8 monitors, so a band that is lively on SSB but quiet on digital and CW can still read low. The Every spot is tagged by direction: ↓ means a monitor in your area heard it, ↑ means your area was heard elsewhere. Heard volume is weighted more heavily than sent volume, because the local monitors are a fixed, always-on instrument while local transmitters come and go; the heard side also gets a flattened time-of-day correction, since the stations it hears follow many clocks, though mostly regional ones. The callsign query folds your own reception reports in through the retrieval API, limited to one query every five minutes under PSK Reporter's rules. If every source fails at once, the browser or network is blocking cross-origin requests; grab the project folder and open index.html locally.
